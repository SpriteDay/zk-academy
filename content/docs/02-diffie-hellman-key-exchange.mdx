## Encrypting messages

Let's imagine we want to have a secret conversation with a friend over the internet. You can agree on a shared key, that you will use to encode your messages. To exchange the private key, you can meet privately. You will share the key, return each to your homes, and text each other using encoded messages

In some sense, that's how we did cryptography for centuries - exchange a shared key privately, and use it to encode the messages. When a message is encrypted, you and your friend are the only ones who can decipher it. You can send a postman with your encoded message, and if a postman will get curious and read it - they won't be able to decrypt it. You can even broadcast your message over the radio - nobody else has a secret key to it, so they won't get the actual content

<Callout>
This approach is called **symmetric-key cryptography** — the same key
locks and unlocks the message. The hard part was never the encryption
itself, but getting that key to your friend without anyone else
seeing it. Cryptographers call this the **key distribution problem**.
</Callout>

But can you agree on a shared key over the same unprotected channel you deliver the encrypted message over? Just to exclude the "meet at the alley privately" part. Sounds quite tough but it is possible, and already was done by your browser as you opened this page

## Sharing secrets publicly

If you send unencrypted messages over the internet - you should consider it is unsafe operation. Your ISP can see whatever requests you made as it delivers them. A server that your message arrives at, is owned by some company. Owner can secretly listen to all incoming traffic, or being infected by spyware. Chances this happening over you asking your friend "how are you doing?", but if you send your bank card details, or discuss something extremely sensitive - someone can have incentive to capture it

So how can we agree on something secret, talking in a public space? We already got one ingredient of this magic - the [modulo operator](/docs/01-what-remainder-remembers), and we need just 1 more

## Integers problems

Some problems are just suck to solve in integers. For example integer factorization. You can easily figure out that 13 multiply by 7 is 91. But how can you figure out what **integers** you need to multiply to get a given number? For example 119. There are no efficient way of doing so, you can make a couple of optimizations, but it's not half as easy as going the other way

<Callout>
[RSA encryption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSA_cryptosystem) actually based on hardness of integer factorization
</Callout>

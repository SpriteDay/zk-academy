In cryptography we base our computations and protocols on **integers**. We do this because fundamental pillars of cryptography such as Number Theory and Boolean Logic work only with discrete numbers

<Callout>
  <>
    Reason for that is actually quite fascinating - when we go from integers to rationals to reals - we loose properties and in some sense "rigidness" of numbers.
    More specifically - going from **integers** to **rational numbers** we loose [prime factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number#Unique_factorization), as we can [define rational numbers by infinite combinations of factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization#:~:text=Factorization%20is%20not,is%20not%20zero)
  </>
</Callout>

Working with discrete numbers is not that different if we compare it to working with rationals or reals (such as `3/5`, `0.16834` or `π`). We can add, multiple and subtract reals and integers just the same. Difference comes when we try to divide integers. If you divide 13 / 5 and want to get an integer... well how do you actually do that?

Interestingly - a CPU on your device knows how to do that and does it as we speak. It simply calculates integer value out of division and checks what is left - getting a remainder. So 13 / 5 is simply:

```
13 / 5 = 2 (and remainder of 3)
```

So you kind of get two operators for division - **modulo** and **full integer division**

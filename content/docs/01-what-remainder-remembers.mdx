In cryptography we base our computations and protocols on integers. We do this because fundamental pillars of cryptography such as Number Theory and Boolean Logic work only with discrete numbers

<Callout>Reason for that is actually quite fascinating - when we go from integers to rationals to reals - we loose properties and in some sense "hardness" of numbers. Reference: TODO </Callout>

Working with discrete numbers is not that different compared to working rationals or reals (3/5 or Pi). We can add, multiple and subtract reals and integers just the same. Difference comes when we try to divide integers. If you divide 12 / 5 and want to get an integer... well how do you actually do that?

Interestingly - a CPU on your device already does it right now. It simply calculates integer value out of division and checks what is left - getting a remainder. So 12 / 5 is simply:

```
12 / 5 = 2 (and remainder of 2)
```

That's how integer division works in CPUs

You kind of get 2 operators for division - modulo and full integer division

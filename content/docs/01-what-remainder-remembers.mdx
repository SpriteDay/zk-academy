In cryptography we base our computations and protocols on **integers**. We do this because fundamental pillars of cryptography such as Number Theory and Boolean Logic work only with discrete values

<Callout>
  <>
    Reason for that is actually quite fascinating - despite integers intuitively being just a "subset" of rational numbers - we actually lose properties going from integers to rationals. Crucially we lose [prime factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number#Unique_factorization) required by core of Number Theory. We lose it because we can define rational numbers by [infinite different combinations of factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization#:~:text=Factorization%20is%20not,is%20not%20zero), while each integer can be defined by only a unique combination of primes
  </>
</Callout>

Working with discrete numbers is not that different compared to working with rational numbers (such as `3/5` or `1.16834`). We can add, multiply and subtract rationals and integers just the same. Difference comes when we try to divide integers. If you divide 13 / 5 and want to get an integer... well how do you actually do that?

While we are speaking, a CPU on your device actually did that a bunch of times. It simply calculates integer value of division and checks what is left - getting a remainder. So 13 / 5 is just:

```
13 / 5 = 2 (and remainder of 3)
```

You kind of get two parts produced by a pair of operators - **full integer division** and **modulo operator** (for getting a remainder)

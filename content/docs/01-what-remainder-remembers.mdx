## How we got to modulo

In cryptography we base our computations and protocols on **integers**. We do this because fundamental pillars of cryptography such as Number Theory and Boolean Logic work only with discrete values

<Callout>
  <>
    Reason for that is actually quite fascinating - despite integers intuitively being just a "subset" of rational numbers - we actually lose properties going from integers to rationals. Crucially we lose [prime factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number#Unique_factorization) required by core of Number Theory. We lose it because we can define rational numbers by [infinite different combinations of factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization#:~:text=Factorization%20is%20not,is%20not%20zero), while each integer can be defined by only a unique combination of primes
  </>
</Callout>

Working with discrete numbers is not that different compared to working with rational numbers (such as `3/5` or `1.16834`). We can add, multiply and subtract rationals and integers just the same. Difference comes when we try to divide integers. If you divide 13 / 5 and want to get an integer... well how do you actually do that?

While we are speaking, a CPU on your device actually did that a bunch of times. It simply calculates integer value of division and checks what is left - getting a remainder. So 13 / 5 is just:

```
13 / 5 = 2 (and remainder of 3)
```

You kind of get two parts produced by a pair of operators - **full integer division** and **modulo operator** (for getting a remainder)

You could actually see how exactly modulo works looking at mechanical clocks:

<HourClock />

You always get an answer from a finite number of integers, though hours keep going by. You get a number from 1 to 12, though it could pass much more than 12 hours

<Callout>Well - to be clear you get a value from 0 to 11 by doing the actual modulo operation, but you see 12 on the clock when the input is cleanly divisible by 12 - that's just how clocks are usually labeled and it is a bit quirky</Callout>

Modulo is essential for cryptography. The interesting property of modulo operation is that it is easy to do in one direction, but impossible to revert. If you count hours, and 42 hours have passed - you know that you will see **6** on the clock. But if someone will steal your clock and they will see 6 hours - they wouldn't know if you waited for 42 hours, or 30 hours, or 18 hours

## Modulo and Primes

Another interesting thing happens when we increase the "step" we do going in modulo clock. Previously we were sliding one hour at a time, let's look at how the modulo clock will behave if we will add more hours per time:

<MultipleHoursPerStepClock />

Do you notice how going 2, 3, 4, and 6 makes the clock "skip" some numbers? I mean if you think about that it's quite logical - for example a step of 3 is basically going a quarter of clock every time. It doesn't matter how long you jump 3 hours ahead - you can't get a number that would get 2 as a result of modulo operation

Same with 4 - you go 1/3 of the clock each time because well - 4 is 1/3 of 12

Example of ones that give you whole spectrum of numbers as result of modulo are:
- Of course number 1 (but it's too boring)
- 5
- 7

You can notice that 5 and 7 don't divide 12 cleanly. This "always off" division is what makes values get pushed as clock keeps going around

Though jumping over numbers feels quite cool - it sucks for properties of the operation. If jumping 3 steps over a 12 hours clock is the same as jumping 1 step over a 4 ticks circle - why pay more?

Let's see how the same interactive clock will behave when we have a prime number as base of the clock. We pick 17 for no particular reason, try to move sliders to see how it will behave now:

<PrimeStepClock />

As you can see, whatever step we pick - it always cover the full numbers range. The reason is - we can't pick such a step that would divide the prime number cleanly, that's literally the only property defining a prime number
